Overview

Pedagogy

Pedagogy refers to the art and science of teaching and learning. The name derives from the Greek word “paidagogos”, which is a combination of the words “paidos”, meaning child, and “agogos”, meaning leader.

Pedagogy emphasises the importance of ongoing discussions between teachers and students throughout the stages of learning, and promotes the development of effective methods such as technology-based learning, in order to adapt instruction to individual student ability. Differentiated instruction, D.I., is the method of tailoring teaching techniques to a student's individual needs and learning styles, counteracting the negative aspects of solo-learning. D.I. also promotes the benefits of gamifying education, especially for younger learners, which evokes transfer learning - the method of applying learned skills to solve a new and unrelated problem, resulting in new skills being developed.

Pedagogy as a whole encourages self-development, journaling, and active reflection as a means of positive and productive learning. Teachers must engage with students, having meaningful conversations in order to kickstart critical thinking. This leads to the authentic understanding of information rather than a general surface knowledge. Overall, pedagogy highlights that the process of teaching and learning should be a collaborative process between teacher and student.

Phonics

Phonics refers to the method of teaching people how to read and write through helping them to recognize and use the different sounds that classify one word from another.

Phonics in music instruction explores the strong correlation between a student's ability to read text and their proficiency in music reading and fluency. It is suggested that early exposure to music and pitch perception is directly related to reading capability in children. Theories based on connecting pitch discrimination and aural recognition are used by music therapists when designing activities, and it is proposed that music and academic success, particularly in reading music while performing, are strongly interconnected. The parallels between decoding in reading text and reading music both involve breaking down information into symbols or individual elements.

Phonemes, crucial for sounding out words, are found to be transferrable between reading and music, and research suggests that the neural encoding areas in the brain for processing sequences, such as sentences and written music, overlap. The shared neural area is believed to contribute to the correlation between musical ability and literacy related skills, with learning musical skills, especially reading music, showing to positively impact brain structure and enhance overall learning ability. It is suggested that there are common learning and processing methods between reading music and text, with children who excel in one often demonstrate increased skills in the other.

Music can be a powerful motivational tool for a positive learning experience, particularly in the context of learning to read.

Pedagogy of the Oppressed

"A teacher's job is “to “fill” the students with the contents of his narration - contents which are detached from reality, disconnected from the totality that engendered them and could give them significance"

p. 71

Narration (with the teacher as narrator) leads the students to memorize mechanically the narrated content. Worse yet, it turns them into "containers", into “receptacles” to be "filled" by the teacher

pp. 71-72

Education thus becomes an act of depositing, in which the students are the depositories, and the teacher is the depositor. Instead of communicating, the teacher issues communiques and makes deposits which the students patiently receive, memorize, and repeat

Knowledge emerges only through invention and re-invention, through the restless, impatient, continuing, hopeful inquiry human beings pursue in the world, with the world, and with each other

p. 72

"The teacher cannot think for her students, nor can she impose her thought on them. Authentic thinking, thinking that is concerned about reality, does not take place in ivory tower isolation, but only in communication"

Liberation is a praxis: the action and reflection of men and women upon their world in order to transform it

p. 79

About problem-posing education, "the teacher is no longer merely the-one-who-teaches, but one who is himself taught in dialogue with the students, who in turn while being taught also teach. They become jointly responsible for a process in which all grow. In this process, arguments based on "authority" are no longer valid; in order to function, authority must be on the side of freedom, not against it"

p. 80

"Banking education resists dialogue; problem-posing education regards dialogue as indispensable to the act of cognition which unveils reality. Banking education treats students as objects of assistance; problem-posing education makes them critical thinkers. Banking education inhibits creativity and domesticates (although it cannot completely destroy) the intentionality of consciousness by isolating consciousness from the world, thereby denying people their ontological and historical vocation of becoming more fully human"

pp. 83-84

"Only dialogue, which requires critical thinking, is also capable of generating critical thinking. Without dialogue there is no communication, and without communication there can be no true education"

p. 93

"Humans, in their permanent relations with reality, produce not only material goods - tangible objects - but also social institutions, ideas, and concepts"

p. 101

"Dialogue does not impose, does not manipulate, does not domesticate, does not "sloganize""

p. 168

"Cultural action is always a systematic and deliberate form of action which operates upon the social structure, either with the objective of preserving that structure or of transforming it"

p. 179

Summary:

A teacher must not only deliver knowledge to a student, but also the significance of the knowledge to allow the student to give their own meaning to it and truly understand it. A one-sided narration from the teacher only leads to memorization from the student, in which they are trained to receive, memorize, and merely repeat. The teacher must create a conversation rather than a narration, for which the student can return questions, inventions and re-inventions, and develop authentic thoughts and understanding of the material. Problem-posing education creates a level-playing-field in which both teacher and student can learn from one another. Banking education is the teaching style familiar to most, wherein the teacher talks and the student listens. Problem-posing education encourages active critical thinking and problem-solving via a two-sided conversation between teacher and student, resulting in creativity and conscious decision-making in cognition. In other words, teaching which requires critical thinking will generate critical thinking. An individual's own understanding of material will create new concepts surrounding it, due to each person's own cultural and unique outlook, resulting in a transformed understanding for all involved.

Music and Literacy, General Music Today, Vol. 21, No. 2

"Although a music teacher's foremost task is to teach music skills, a child's ability to read text has important implications for his or her success in learning to read music"

The magic of music can entice a child to practice various reading-related tasks, often without the child even being aware of the learning objective

  • Reading problems occur at the level of the single word because of failure to quickly decode visual information
  • Decoding is dependent on sensitivity to the sound structure of language rather than on comprehension. That is, a child recognizes that words rhyme or sound different, a skill that is separate from recognizing the word's meaning
  • To decode language information, alphabet letter recognition must be paired with phonetic sound patterns to acquire skills in sound blending and word segmentation
  • Attention, memory, and comprehension skills derive from the above

"It is widely believed that music learning, music reading, and music participation enhance academic achievement, especially reading and math (Tucker, 1981)"

A child's ability to read correlates to music-reading ability and fluency. Music can be used as an enjoyable vessel for teaching/learning. Reading ability stems from recognition of sound structure, that is, acknowledgement of letter recognition matching to a sound pattern. It is suggested that early-years pitch perception is directly related to reading capability in childhood, and that music correlates to proficiency in reading ability.

"There is evidence that there is some overlap, for example, in processing rhythmic structure, rhyme awareness, or in speech perception (Corriveau et al., 2010; Liu et al., 2010; Moreno & Besson, 2006)"

"In a study looking at the processing of speech regularities in children aged 8-13, reading and music aptitude had positive correlations with auditory working memory (Strait et al., 2011)"

"There is a direct relationship between musical skill and literacy related skills as a result of having a common encoding mechanism"

"Research evidence from functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) indicates that there are indeed changes in brain structure that result from musical training that may lead to an improved capacity to learn (Ragert et al., 2004; Stewart, 2003, 2008)"

p. 335

"The reading of musical notation has indeed been found to show a positive impact on brain function (Stewart & Williamon, 2008; Stewart et al., 2003). Here, again there is the likelihood of a shared mechanism. Reading music requires perception of, and encoding of, visual symbols followed by the action of producing a musical response. Language reading requires perception of, and encoding of, visual symbols followed by the action of producing a verbal response"

"Reading music shares similarities with reading text; the visual appearance of symbols on a stave bear as little resemblance to their musical sounds as letters do to spoken sounds"

"In Stewart's study (2003) after only 3 months of training, novice pianists had started to process musical notation automatically. Scans revealed that particular areas of the brain were activated whilst reading musical notation that could not be detected in the control group. Simply seeing musical notes after a short period of time set in motion a sequence of neural events related to the learned musical response (Stewart & Williamon, 2008)"

p. 336

Summary:

Evidence suggests that language and music utilise the same neural encoding area of the brain in relation to processing sequences, i.e., sentences, bars of written music. It is suggested that rhythm processing and speech perception share this neural area, and thus there is direct correlation between musical ability and aural functional memory - specifically literacy related skills. Evidence from research indicates that learning musical skills changes brain structure in a way that improves learning ability in general. In particular, reading music has shown increased brain function, again indicated the shared brain function. Reading music and reading text also shares a perception and encoding of visual symbols, subsequently followed by a musical or verbal response. Similarly, both musical notation and letters do not resemble their corresponding sounds. Another study found that those who had some ability in playing the piano and were able to sight-read musical notation utilised areas of the brain that those who could not read music were not able to utilise. Conversely, it could be argued that any new skill utilises areas of the brain that would otherwise not be used.
